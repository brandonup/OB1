# Brain — Contextual Intelligence

All of Brain's "smart" behaviors — reminding you of something you forgot, catching a gap in your thinking, connecting dots across domains — are expressions of a single underlying capability: **contextual intelligence.** The system knows something relevant that the user hasn't asked about, recognizes that it's relevant *right now*, and decides to surface it at the moment of action.

The nine behaviors below are all flavors of this capability. What varies is timing, tone, and what gets surfaced.

---

## 1. Proactive Recall

The system surfaces a stored fact the user forgot they had. The fact already exists in Brain — the user just didn't think to ask for it, and the system recognized it was relevant to what they're doing right now.

**Example:** You're writing an email to Sam about the widget project and list 3 items. Brain surfaces a 4th item from yesterday's email scan that you didn't mention — not because you asked, but because it recognized the relevance.

---

## 2. Proactive Insights

The system connects dots across multiple stored facts to notice a pattern the user hasn't seen. This goes beyond recalling a single fact — it synthesizes across records to generate a new observation.

**Example:** "You've added 4 tasks with Friday deadlines this week, but your calendar shows back-to-back meetings Thursday and Friday. You may want to reprioritize."

---

## 3. Contextual Surfacing

The system pulls in relevant context because of what the user is currently doing, not because they asked for it. The trigger is the user's current activity, and the system enriches that activity with what it knows.

**Example:** You mention "Sarah" in a conversation. Brain surfaces what it knows about Sarah — her role, your last interaction, any open items — without you asking "Who is Sarah?"

---

## 4. Gentle Correction

The system notices the user missed something or made a minor error and volunteers the correction without being pushy. The tone matters here — it's a nudge, not a challenge.

**Example:** You ask Brain to draft a follow-up email saying "as discussed in our Monday meeting." Brain notes: "Your calendar shows the meeting with this group was on Tuesday, not Monday — want me to adjust?"

---

## 5. Gap Detection

The system recognizes that something is *absent* from the user's request that should be there, based on what it knows. This is different from gentle correction (something wrong) — this is about something missing.

**Example:** You're preparing for a client call and list your talking points. Brain notices you didn't mention the pricing change that was decided last week and flags it: "You may want to address the pricing update from the March 28 decision."

---

## 6. Contextual Priming

The system pre-loads relevant context *before* the user starts working, based on what it anticipates they'll need. This happens before any task begins — the system prepares the ground so context is already available when the user sits down.

**Example:** The daily email scan runs at 8am, processes yesterday's messages, and updates the project summary and task list. When you open Brain at 9am, the context is already fresh — you don't need to catch up manually.

---

## 7. Contradiction Detection

The system notices when two stored facts conflict and flags the inconsistency for the user to resolve. This prevents Brain from confidently surfacing outdated or wrong information.

**Example:** "You recorded a decision to go with Vendor A on March 15, but yesterday's email thread discusses onboarding with Vendor B. These may conflict — which is current?"

---

## 8. Temporal Awareness

The system understands that facts have a shelf life and adjusts confidence accordingly. Without this, proactive recall can surface stale context with false confidence — which is worse than not surfacing it at all.

**Example:** "You mentioned Sam was leading the migration, but that was 6 weeks ago and there have been no references since. This may have changed — want me to check recent signals?"

---

## 9. Cross-Domain Bridging

The system connects information across domains that the user hasn't explicitly linked. This is the highest-value form of contextual intelligence and the most sensitive, because it intentionally crosses the domain boundaries Brain otherwise enforces. It should only happen when the connection is genuinely useful and clearly cited.

**Example:** "Your reflection last week about feeling overwhelmed maps to the 4 tasks you added with the same deadline. Your goal of 'improving work-life boundaries' may be relevant here — want to review?"
